"A device to analyze muscle stimulatory response to determine the precise location at which to operate for anorectal malformation cases."

A. Introduction

Anorectal malformation is among the more common congenital diseases, with a prevalence of 1 in 2000 to 1 in 5000 live births. The current solutions are expensive, and as the companies that make the devices went out of business, the machines used for the surgery cannot be adequately serviced. This leaves a unique situation where only a few major hospitals across entire countries are the only ones left with the devices, and patients require travel to these specific hospitals to have this surgery performed. The current technology used in the surgery works by sending an electrical impulse, this in turn causes muscular contraction which the physician will observe to determine the site of the incision. Unfortunately, this is a subjective approach and could result in dire consequences in the case of inaccuracies in choosing an incision site. Our technology hopes to remove the subjectivity from the equation and instead read the strength of the electrical feedback from the muscular response to objectively determine the site of incision.

B. Project Objective

Primarily, it is important to remove subjectivity from the surgical process and to provide the surgeon with a more objective view. It is expected at this stage that we will have prototypes of this response analyzer device design made of some cheap materials such as craft or plastics. Previously, Dr. De la Torre’s lab has developed a functioning electrical stimulation device that can elicit a clear response from the muscles. Therefore, we wish to develop a device to read and interpret these signals and calculate the center of the sphincter to assist the surgeon in making the incision at the proper location. If accomplished successfully, this device will improve the accuracy of the surgery. The final deliverable would be a device that can both carry out the stimulation, analyze the tissue response, and provide the surgeon with the location of the sphincter’s center.

C. Assumptions

We are progressing on the assumption that the electrical stimulation method works, otherwise we would need to consider another method of tissue analysis, such as an optical method. We assume we will be able to breadboard this circuit and have access to necessary materials such as the board, resistors, wires, etc. Finally, we assume we may test this circuit on tissue that would provide response, such as a chicken or pig (deceased) muscle tissue and assume that the signal strength would be sufficient for the device to read, although it may not necessarily be representative of the signal resulting from human muscular contraction, and would need to be tested further.

D. Project Scope

We plan on further testing this device to be compatible with our simulated waveforms. Importantly, it should be able to determine the intensities of the inputs on either “side” of the center to identify the location of the center point. The bounds of this are limited by the capabilities of available waveform generator devices, and waveforms that are step, sinusoidal, ramp or square. This does stop short of using various tissue samples of deceased animals, which we anticipate will be the next step and require a new charter be signed.

Requirements

Future goals which are currently outside the scope of the project are live animal testing prior to testing the device on humans. This will likely need Pitt IRB approval. This is an important stage to the device due to its eventual use in surgical procedures on humans, but is not currently within the scope of this project.
